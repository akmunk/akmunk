Together with Torben Elgaard Jensen, Andreas Birkbak and Anders Koed Madsen, I wrote a chapter for the MIT Press collection _Making & Doing: Activating STS through Knowledge Expression and Travel_, edited by Gary Downey and Teun Zuiderent-Jerak. The chapter was our attempt to put into words something we had been doing for a while without quite having a name for it - what we ended up calling **participatory data design** (PDD).

The chapter grew directly out of a series of data sprints we had been running since around 2015. If you haven't come across data sprints before, the basic idea is a short, intensive workshop - typically three to five days - where researchers and external stakeholders sit down together to collect, wrangle, and make sense of digital data around a shared problem. The format itself has a longer history (shoutout to colleagues like Tommaso Venturini and Axel Meunier who helped develop it), but in TANTlab we were also drawing heavily on the **Scandinavian participatory design tradition** - the idea, going back to the 1980s labour movement, that the people who will actually use a technology should have a real say in how it gets designed. We wanted to ask what that spirit might look like when the "technology" in question is a data analysis, and the future users are citizens, municipal administrators, or theatre directors.

The chapter opens with a story that captures both the promise and the weirdness of this kind of work pretty well. John - a very experienced organizer at the Danish Board of Technology - found himself staring at a poster-sized hairball of Twitter hashtags about the Zika virus, trying to figure out what any of it had to do with his citizen hearing on pandemics. Spoiler: it eventually worked out, but not in the way anyone expected. The group ended up inventing something they half-jokingly called "the John score" - essentially asking John to just read tweets and rate their relevance based on his gut feeling. Elegant? Maybe not. Useful? Absolutely.

Beyond the storytelling, we tried to map out three practices that kept coming up across our projects: **datafication** (figuring out what data even makes sense for a given problem), **flexible visualization** (using data maps as objects people can argue around), and **materialization** (actually building something at the end). None of these were clean or predictable, and we were pretty honest about the moments where things went sideways - including the uncomfortable realization that our visualizations could be grabbed and weaponized by whoever happened to be in power.

The chapter is part of an open-access volume, so you can read the whole thing for free over at MIT Press.

This book chapter began as a pet project during the early days of COVID lockdown in 2020. Forced to work from home, but with no established practice for meeting or teaching virtually yet, there was time to experiment. I decided to follow the trends for COVID-related searches across all national versions of Google.

> [!quote] p.281
> One of the things that truly puzzles me when we discuss internet behavior in relation to ongoing controversies is the passivity we attribute to the public as consumers of information. (...) As I have shown above, search traces from Google give reason to believe that political and cultural geographies have played a role in how interest in coronavirus has been articulated across different countries. In short, this means that coronavirus has not merely been something that Google users were interested in, but also something that prompted Google users to take an interest in a range of other matters. Their interest in coronavirus can therefore potentially be seen as a transformation of existing political identities — and perhaps even the beginning of new ones. This calls for us to attribute a far more active and co-producing role to the corona-interested publics of the internet than infodemiology typically implies.
